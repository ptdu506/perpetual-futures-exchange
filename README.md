## What Actually Makes an Exchange Good for Perpetual Futures

Before I name names, let me be honest about the criteria, because a lot of "best exchange" guides just list features without weighting them.

**Liquidity is king, and it's invisible until it isn't.** On paper every exchange has "deep liquidity." In practice, the difference between a $10 million and a $20 million order book within 0.1% of mid-price is the difference between a clean fill and slippage that wrecks your risk-reward on a scalp. For BTC/USDT perps, the very top exchanges regularly show double-digit millions per side; mid-tier venues do not.

**Fees compound, but only past a certain volume.** Below roughly $500K a month in notional, the fee gap between most major exchanges is noise — maybe $10–$50 a month. Above $1M, it becomes real money, and above $10M it's the difference between a profitable and unprofitable strategy. So fee optimization matters, but mostly for active traders, and it matters more on the maker side if you're a limit-order person.

**Leverage is a marketing number, not a strategy.** Every exchange advertises 100x or 125x. That doesn't mean you should use it. A 0.5% move against a 200x position is a full liquidation. The real question is whether the exchange offers sensible intermediate leverage tiers (5x, 10x, 25x) with clean margin controls.

**Unified account vs. separate wallets is the most underrated differentiator.** This is the one nobody talks about in listicles. Some exchanges make you manually transfer funds between a spot wallet, a futures wallet, and a funding wallet every time you want to do something. Others pool everything — your spot BTC automatically counts as collateral for a USDT-margined perp. That friction, or lack of it, compounds over weeks of trading.

**Regulation is the risk that bites slowly.** Offshore exchanges have faced moving regulatory lines repeatedly. The question isn't just "can I use it today" but "will I still be able to withdraw next year from where I live."
